Shifty Movement
How evolution slipped past the fitness function.

Creature 900423 slides across the ground at 5 m/s with zero joint actuation. It has 12 joints, 0 degrees of freedom, 0 effectors — yet it scored in the 99th percentile. This explains exactly how, and why the safety net designed to catch it had a gap big enough to drive a truck through.

Discovery #1: every joint is rigid.

When I inspected the creature's genotype, all 11 genome nodes specified JointType::Rigid. The phenotype's 12 joints are every one a FixedJoint in Rapier — welds, not hinges. This creature has no moving parts. The brain has no effectors to drive, because there are no degrees of freedom to drive.

Discovery #2: gravity is generous.

Land creatures spawn at y = 2.0 and fall under gravity until they touch the ground. For a creature whose center of mass ends up at y ≈ 0.24, that's a drop of 1.76 m. The potential energy released has to go somewhere — and with the right geometry, most of it becomes horizontal kinetic energy.

FREE-FALL VELOCITY

Potential energy at spawn height, converted fully to kinetic:

mgh = ½mv²
v = √(2gh)
= √(2 × 9.81 × 1.76)
= 5.87 m/s

Upper bound: the full speed achievable from pure free-fall conversion, ignoring losses.

OBSERVED PEAK VELOCITY

From the trace, max measured second-over-second drift:

Peak 5.9 m/s between t=5–6 s, then a slow decay to 5.1 m/s as friction bleeds energy. The match with the theoretical bound is exact. Nothing else is needed to explain the motion.

WHAT'S ACTUALLY HAPPENING

1. Creature spawns as a rigid welded assembly at y = 2.0 (13 flat plates glued together).
2. Gravity pulls it down. Kinetic energy builds up during the fall: ≈ 5.87 m/s worth.
3. The assembly's flat, asymmetric geometry hits the ground at an angle.
4. The impulse from ground collision deflects angular momentum into translational motion.
5. The thin-plate shape presents low friction area per unit mass — the assembly skates.
6. Friction slowly bleeds energy, but over the 10-second window the creature slides 44 m.

This is not a physics bug. Given the geometry, this is what Rapier is supposed to simulate. The bug is that evolution found this and the fitness function couldn't penalise it.

The escape hatch: min_joint_motion skips DOF-less creatures.

The fitness layer already has an anti-exploit mechanism — a per-DOF windowed stddev check that multiplies the score by a coefficient near 0 when joints stay frozen for any 2-second window. It is designed to catch the classic "pin a joint at its limit and push" exploit. But it has a guard clause at the top that never runs when the creature has zero DOFs.

INTENT

"A creature with no joints (one body) gets coefficient 1.0 — the penalty only applies when there IS a brain→joint control path that could exploit."   — the doc comment in fitness.rs

WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS

Intent says "single-body creature". Code says dof_index.is_empty(). Those are different! A 13-body creature whose joints are all Rigid has empty dof_index too — and it gets motion_coef = 1.0, same as a single body. No penalty, no question.

Selection locked on at generation 20.

Island 2's max fitness climbs honestly for 19 generations — small random walkers, score edging up around 0.1 per generation. Then at generation 20 it jumps 6× in a single step. That's not gradual refinement; that's a mutation discovering the rigid-skate exploit. Once discovered, selection locks on: the champion clones itself forward every generation without re-evaluation.

Migration spread the exploit to 4 of 5 islands.

The evolution runs a ring-topology islands model: every 20 generations the best creature of each island is shipped to the next. The fix: distinguish single-body from rigid-assembly.

The guard clause conflates two genuinely different cases. A single-body creature cannot exploit joints because there are no joints. A multi-body creature with all-Rigid joints is a different animal: it's a complex shape whose motion comes entirely from physics-solver behaviour, not from brain control. The first deserves the pass. The second deserves a zero.

core/src/fitness.rs:316 — proposed
let num_bodies = creature.world.bodies.len(); let motion_coef = match (params.min_joint_motion, num_bodies == 1, dof_index.is_empty()) { (None, _, _) => 1.0, // feature disabled → paper-faithful (_, true, _) => 1.0, // single body truly cannot exploit (Some(_), false, true) => 0.0, // multi-body + 0 DOFs → rigid-skate exploit (Some(threshold), false, false) => { // existing per-window stddev check ... } };
WHY THIS IS THE RIGHT LAYER

Per CLAUDE.md: "Do NOT modify physics code." The physics engine is correct — a flat rigid assembly can legitimately skate on a frictional ground plane. We don't want to tamper with that. The bug is at the fitness filtering layer: Sims' paper evaluates creatures whose locomotion comes from actuated joint motion. A creature with no actuators is outside that scope and should score zero when the anti-exploit guard is active.

BACKWARDS COMPATIBILITY

Setting min_joint_motion: None keeps the original paper-faithful behaviour (no penalty, no zero). Users who want passive-geometry experiments opt out. Users who want the standard "evolve creatures that locomote via neural control" get the rigid-skate exploit killed at birth. The default (Some(0.3)) becomes correct rather than full-of-holes.

Summary

Creature 900423 didn't break physics. It found a shape that converts gravitational potential energy into horizontal sliding, and a fitness function that couldn't tell the difference between "single body, can't exploit" and "rigid assembly, can exploit plenty". Evolution did what evolution always does: found the crack and drove through it.

WHAT EVOLUTION DID

Discovered that JointType::Rigid makes all brain-control irrelevant, then optimised body geometry for low-friction sliding. Four out of five islands converged on the same exploit via ring-topology migration.

WHAT WENT WRONG

The min_joint_motion guard used dof_index.is_empty() as a proxy for "creature can't exploit joints". That test is true for single-body creatures (correct) AND for all-Rigid multi-body creatures (wrong).

WHAT TO CHANGE

One match arm. Give single-body creatures the free pass; zero out multi-body-with-no-DOFs. Physics stays untouched; the fitness filter distinguishes the cases it was always meant to distinguish.

THE META-LESSON

Evolutionary search treats every line of fitness code as a specification of what to maximise. The dof_index.is_empty() guard read — to selection pressure — as "creatures with no DOFs are exempt from the anti-exploit check". Selection did not ask whether that was the author's intent. It only asked whether the condition could be triggered, and whether triggering it improved score. Once the answer was "yes" to both, the exploit propagated through the entire island mesh in under 30 generations.
